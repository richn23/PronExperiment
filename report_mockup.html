How your score is calculated

Built on Microsoft Azure Speech AI · calibrated for English L2 learners

The data source

Every recording is sent to Azure Speech Services Pronunciation Assessment — the same speech AI used by Microsoft Reading Coach and a number of major language-learning platforms. It returns scores per individual sound, per syllable, and per sentence, plus pacing and intonation signals.

The five dimensions

DimensionWeightWhat it measures
Phoneme accuracy25%Are the individual sounds correct?
Fluency22%Does speech flow at a natural pace, without unnatural pauses?
Word stress18%Is the right syllable emphasised in each word?
Consistency18%Is performance steady across items, or does it swing?
Sentence stability17%Does pronunciation hold up in sentences vs single words?

The formula

Score = (Phoneme × 0.25)
        + (Fluency × 0.22)
        + (Stress × 0.18)
        + (Consistency × 0.18)
        + (Stability × 0.17)

Why a composite, not a single number?

Two learners can both score 80 overall — one through consistently good performance, one through a mix of strong and weak items. Listeners experience these very differently. The five dimensions make that difference visible, and let learners see where to focus, not just how well they did.

What you can take from this

On most multi-syllable words you placed stress correctly — your stress placement instinct is good. The notable exception was electricity, where you stressed the final syllable instead of "TRIC". This is a common pattern: word-final syllables naturally lengthen in English, but the stress should still land on TRIC. Practising this word with deliberate stress on the third syllable (e-lec-TRIC-i-ty) would help.

About this view

Bar widths show how long you held each syllable. In English, the stressed syllable is naturally longer than the unstressed ones. By comparing your timing against the expected stress pattern, we can see whether your stress placement matches a native speaker's.

Note: word-final syllables can sometimes appear longest due to natural lengthening at the end of a word. The system compensates for this when judging stress placement.
